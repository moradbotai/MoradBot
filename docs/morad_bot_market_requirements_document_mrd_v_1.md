# MoradBot — Market Requirements Document (MRD)

## Market-Focused Analysis (No Technical Solutions)

---

### 1. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

The ideal customer for MoradBot is not "any e-commerce merchant". The ICP must be narrowly defined to avoid dilution and weak product-market fit.

**Primary ICP:**

Saudi-based Salla merchants with:
- 30–300 orders per month
- 1–3 people managing the store
- No dedicated customer support team
- Receiving daily repetitive questions via website chat or WhatsApp
- Operating in competitive categories (beauty, fashion, supplements, electronics accessories)

**Psychographic Traits:**
- Time-constrained
- Cost-sensitive but willing to invest in tools that save time
- Not highly technical
- Frustrated with repetitive manual work
- Wants growth but feels operationally overwhelmed

**Not the ICP:**
- Large stores with full support teams
- Very small stores (<10 orders/month) with minimal inquiries
- Dropshippers who do not control inventory data

The true ICP is the "solo or micro-team merchant under operational pressure."

---

### 2. Core Pain Point (And Why It Hurts)

The central pain point is not "customer support automation." That is too generic.

The real pain is:

> Constant interruption from repetitive questions that consume mental bandwidth and delay business growth tasks.

These merchants:
- Answer the same 5–10 questions repeatedly.
- Switch context dozens of times daily.
- Delay product sourcing, marketing, and optimization tasks.

Why it is painful:

1. Time fragmentation reduces productivity.
2. Delayed responses reduce conversion rates.
3. Manual replies create burnout.
4. Slow response harms brand trust.

However, this pain is moderate—not catastrophic. It is frustrating but not business-ending.

The pain intensifies when:
- The merchant scales above 50+ orders/month.
- Questions increase but hiring support is too expensive.
- Response delay starts impacting conversion.

---

### 3. Existing Market Solutions (And Why They Are Insufficient)

Current alternatives include:

1. Manual WhatsApp responses
   - Free but time-consuming
   - No automation

2. Hiring part-time support
   - Expensive relative to revenue
   - Requires training and supervision

3. Simple canned replies/macros
   - Still manual
   - No dynamic product data

4. Generic chatbot builders
   - Often rule-based and rigid
   - Require configuration complexity
   - Poor Arabic quality in many cases

5. Ignoring chat completely
   - Leads to lost conversions

Why these solutions fail the ICP:
- Too expensive
- Too complex
- Too rigid
- Not adapted to Arabic conversational nuance

However, it must be stated honestly:
The merchant already survives without MoradBot.
This means switching cost must be justified.

---

### 4. Trigger to Pay (When They Actually Decide)

Merchants do not pay for automation out of curiosity.
They pay when:

1. They miss sales due to slow replies.
2. They feel overwhelmed answering repetitive questions.
3. They calculate the cost of their own time.
4. They experience a growth plateau caused by operational bottlenecks.

The strongest trigger is:

> "I am spending too much time answering the same questions and it is limiting my growth."

Secondary trigger:

> "My competitor replies faster than me."

Without one of these triggers, adoption will be weak.

---

### 5. Market Risks

1. Problem Severity Risk
   - The pain may not be strong enough for many merchants.
   - Many tolerate manual replies.

2. Platform Risk
   - Dependence on Salla ecosystem.
   - If Salla launches native AI support, differentiation collapses.

3. Price Sensitivity Risk
   - Micro-merchants are highly cost-sensitive.
   - Even 49–99 SAR may feel optional.

4. Education Risk
   - Merchants may not understand AI value.
   - Requires clear ROI demonstration.

5. Trust Risk
   - Fear of wrong answers.
   - Fear of brand damage.

6. Feature Creep Risk
   - Pressure to expand beyond FAQ quickly.

---

### 6. Is This a Real Problem or "Nice to Have"?

Honest Assessment:

For merchants below ~30 orders/month:
- Nice to Have.

For merchants 30–150 orders/month:
- Moderate problem.
- Strong when growth begins.

For merchants 150+ orders/month without support staff:
- Real operational pain.

Conclusion:

The problem is real but segment-dependent.
It is not universally urgent.
It becomes urgent at specific growth thresholds.

If positioned incorrectly, MoradBot becomes a "nice automation toy."
If positioned correctly (time savings + conversion impact), it can become a practical operational tool.

---

Final Verdict:

MoradBot addresses a real but non-catastrophic operational friction.
Its success depends entirely on targeting merchants who are actively scaling and operationally strained.

Without tight ICP targeting, adoption will stall.

End of MRD
